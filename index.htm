LOGCAP

LOGCAP (Logistics Civil Augmentation Program)

  • Created in 1985 to privatize more of the duties involved in civil logistics in the military.
  • Later, Dick Cheny, as defense secretary in 1992, spearheaded a movement to privatize most of the military's civil logistics activities.
  • Under his direction, Pentagon paid $9 million to Halliburton/KBR to conduct a study to determine whether private companies like itself should handle all of the military's civil logistics.
  • KBR's classified study concluded that greater privatization of logistics was in the government's best interest. Shortly after, on Aug 3, 1992, Cheney awarded the first comprehensive LOGCAP contract to KBR.
  • 3 years later, Halliburton hired Cheney as CEO
  • In 1997, two years after Cheney became CEO of Halliburton, KBR's LOGCAP contract was not renewed; the government alleged the company engaged in fraudulent billing practices.
  • The independent auditing arm of Congress, the GAO, criticized KBR's performance during America's war in the Balkans. KBR's cost-overruns in the Balkans inflated the original contract price by 32%.
  • After KBR was fired by the Army in 1997, the LOGCAP contract was awarded to Halliburton competitor DynCorp. But after Cheney became Vice President in 2001, DynCorp was fired and KBR was re-awarded the contract.
  • Halliburton revenue from LOGCAP increased from $320 million in 2nd quarter of 2003 to over $2 billion in fourth quarter of 2003.
  • As of May 2004, the federal government had spent nearly $5 billion on LOGCAP since KBR became the sole contractor in 2001.
  • The original value of LOGCAP in Iraq was estimated at over $4 billion, but the value of the contract is now over $8 billion. $8 billion figure for Iraq does not include KBR's LOGCAP business in dozens of other countries around the globe, including Guantanimo Bay, Cuba, where it constructed the prison facilities.
